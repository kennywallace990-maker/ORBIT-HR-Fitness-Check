Executive Overview

This report provides a comprehensive, AI-driven thematic analysis of the 2025 employee listening data across the Core Fulfillment Center (FC) Network. Drawing from 19,058 distinct TM signals across 13 sites, its purpose is to distill raw feedback into actionable intelligence. By isolating the top five cultural strengths and the bottom five operational opportunities, this document is designed to equip the Senior Leadership Team (SLT) with the exact context needed to formulate targeted, high-impact action plans for 2026.

II. Top 5 Positive Themes (Strengths to Leverage)

1. Visible, Approachable Frontline Leadership

Signals: ~1,379 | Network-wide

When TMs across the network describe what makes a good day at work, the answer consistently centers on one factor: whether their direct leadership showed up, not just physically, but functionally. The 1,379 leadership-related signals represent more than appreciation; they represent a clear organizational signal about what TMs need to perform.

The data reveals that TMs don't praise "management" in the abstract. They praise specific behaviors: an AM who noticed a bottleneck and reassigned labor before it cascaded; an OM who walked the floor during a difficult shift and helped troubleshoot a jammed conveyor; a lead who advocated for a TM struggling with a new process. These are not leadership platitudes, they're operational interventions that TMs notice and remember.

Sites with the highest leadership signal density (RNO1 at 207 signals, AVP2 at 189, HOU1 at 149) share a common trait: their leaders are on the floor during peak friction moments, not sequestered in offices reviewing metrics. The correlation is clear, when leaders are visible, TMs feel supported. When leaders are invisible, every operational frustration compounds.

Insight

Leadership development for 2026 should not focus on abstract management competencies. It should focus on floor presence cadence, real-time problem-solving capability, and the discipline to prioritize TM-facing time over administrative tasks. The sites that get this right will outperform.

2. Micro-Recognition & Rewards Culture

Signals: ~825 | Network-wide

The 825 recognition signals tell a story about what motivates hourly workers in a physically demanding environment: it's not grand gestures, but small, consistent acknowledgments that they're seen.

The pin program, Chewy Points, and site-level raffles have become cultural touchstones. TMs mention these programs with genuine enthusiasm, not because the rewards are materially significant, but because they represent proof that someone noticed their effort. In an environment where the work is repetitive and physically taxing, these micro-recognitions break the monotony and signal that individual contribution matters.

MCO1 stands as the network's recognition exemplar, with 162 recognition signals, seven times higher than most sites. The site's secret isn't a different program; it's frequency and authenticity. MCO1 captures 762 signals through Informal Conversations (highest in network), creating natural moments for recognition to occur organically. Recognition at MCO1 isn't a scheduled event, it's embedded in daily leader-TM interaction.

However, the data also reveals a blind spot. TMs in support roles, Box Builders, Water Spiders, stagers in Pack/Ship, consistently report feeling excluded from recognition systems designed around picker productivity metrics. When Chewy Points flow disproportionately to Pickers, support roles perceive a hierarchy of value that undermines teamwork. Several signals explicitly state: "We make their rate possible, but we don't get the points."

Insight

The recognition infrastructure works. The question for 2026 is whether it's reaching everyone who deserves it. Expanding micro-reward allocation to indirect paths isn't about fairness optics, it's about recognizing the full operational system that enables performance.

3. Chewy Culture & The Onboarding Experience

Signals: ~497 | Network-wide

The onboarding data tells two stories, one encouraging, one cautionary.

The encouraging story: New hires arrive enthusiastic. Week 3 surveys consistently show 90%+ Happy sentiment. TMs cite Chewy's culture, competitive wages, and schedule flexibility as primary reasons for joining. The recruitment pipeline is successfully selling an authentic value proposition, and new hires feel it immediately. Comments like "Everything was great, I can't think of anything to improve" are common in early-tenure feedback.

The cautionary story: That enthusiasm is fragile. The 497 signals in this category are overwhelmingly positive, but they're also overwhelmingly concentrated in the first 30 days. The organizational challenge isn't attracting talent, it's retaining the goodwill that new hires bring through the door.

The data suggests a predictable decay curve. Week 3 sentiment is high because new hires haven't yet encountered the operational friction that tenured TMs live with daily, equipment shortages, facility issues, policy confusion, rotation stagnation. By Month 3, the honeymoon ends. By Month 6, the TM either adapts to the reality or begins mentally exiting.

Small breakdowns in the onboarding process, repeated drug test requirements, confusion on start dates, inconsistent training experiences, don't just cause Day 1 frustration. They signal to new hires that the organizational precision they expected may not exist. Every pre-Day-1 friction point erodes the cultural trust that recruiting worked hard to build.

Insight

Onboarding success should be measured not at Week 3, but at Month 6. The 2026 priority is building a bridge from new-hire optimism to sustainable engagement, which means addressing the operational realities that test that early goodwill.

4. Schedule Predictability

Signals: ~362 | Network-wide

In an industry where gig work and unpredictable scheduling have become normalized, Chewy's commitment to stable shift structures is a genuine differentiator, and TMs notice.

The 362 signals in this category reveal that schedule predictability isn't a perk for FC workers; it's a foundational need. TMs describe the ability to plan childcare, second jobs, and personal commitments around a reliable schedule as one of Chewy's core value propositions. For hourly workers managing complex lives, knowing when you work next week isn't a luxury, it's the difference between stability and chaos.

This strength is particularly resonant with new hires, who often arrive from employers with erratic scheduling practices. The contrast is immediate and appreciated. Several signals explicitly compare Chewy favorably to previous employers: "I actually know my schedule in advance" is a recurring sentiment.

However, the policy section of this report (Theme #4 in Opportunities) reveals the tension beneath this strength. While core schedules are predictable, the allocation of VTO and VET is not. TMs who value schedule stability become frustrated when they can't access voluntary time off, or when overtime opportunities seem to favor certain individuals. The foundational expectation of predictability creates a higher standard for transparency in all time-related decisions.

Insight

Schedule predictability is a competitive advantage worth protecting. But that advantage erodes when VTO/VET allocation feels opaque or unfair. The 2026 opportunity is extending the clarity of core scheduling into the discretionary time systems.

5. Peer-to-Peer Teamwork & Support Roles

Signals: ~354 | Network-wide

The 354 teamwork signals reveal something important about how TMs experience the FC floor: they think in systems, not silos.

When TMs express gratitude, they often name specific peers whose work directly enables their own: the Water Spider who kept their station stocked during a rush, the Box Builder whose prep work allowed them to hit rate, the cross-shift counterpart who left their area set up for success. These aren't generic "teamwork is great" sentiments, they're specific acknowledgments of operational interdependence.

This systems-thinking has implications for how recognition and performance management should work. When a Picker hits an exceptional rate, that rate was enabled by a network of support roles. When recognition flows only to the visible end-point (the Picker), the enabling network feels invisible. Several signals make this explicit: TMs want their supporting cast recognized, not because of altruism, but because they understand that their own success depends on that cast showing up tomorrow.

The cross-shift handoff mentions are particularly notable. TMs who arrive to a clean, organized station feel set up for success; TMs who arrive to chaos feel abandoned. The quality of shift transitions directly impacts both productivity and morale. Sites that invest in handoff protocols and cross-shift communication are building operational resilience.

Insight

Recognition and performance systems should acknowledge operational nodes, not just individual metrics. Consider piloting team-based recognition that honors the picker-stager-water spider unit as a system. The TMs already think this way; the recognition systems should catch up.

III. Bottom 5 Opportunity Themes (Areas for 2026 Action)

1. Safety Standard Gaps & Hazard Exposure

Signals: ~3,017

Safety emerges as the most frequently flagged concern in the dataset, but the story these 3,017 signals tell is more nuanced than "TMs feel unsafe." The real narrative is about perceived organizational consistency: TMs believe in Safety First as a value, but they experience daily contradictions that erode that belief.

The tension is operational. TMs feel pressure to hit rate while navigating physical hazards that slow them down. A damaged pallet in a pick location creates a choice: flag it and lose time, or work around it and risk injury. When TMs choose to flag hazards and those hazards persist for days, the message received is that throughput matters more than safety.

The specific breakdown of hazard mentions reveals where the friction concentrates. PPE availability (251 mentions), pallet hazards (132), and PIT/forklift incidents (129) lead the categories.

PHX1 (12.4% safety rate), MCO1 (10.1%), and CFC1 (9.5%) carry disproportionate safety signal density. These sites need immediate intervention, not because TMs are complaining, but because the elevated signal rate is a leading indicator of potential incidents.

Insight

TMs aren't asking for safety theater; they're asking for visible resolution. When a hazard is reported, TMs need to see it addressed within 24-48 hours. The 2026 priority isn't more safety messaging, it's closing the gap between Safety First as a stated value and Safety First as an operational reality.

2. Equipment Unreliability & Tool Scarcity

Signals: ~2,955

The 2,955 equipment signals represent something more fundamental than operational friction: they represent a perceived broken contract. TMs understand they're held to high performance standards, rate, quality, attendance. What they can't accept is being held to those standards while denied the basic tools to meet them.

The frustration in these signals is palpable. A TM who can't find a working scanner isn't mildly inconvenienced, they're watching their rate crater while searching for functional equipment. A picker whose cart has a broken wheel isn't experiencing a minor annoyance, they're doing more physical work for the same output. The cumulative effect is a workforce that feels set up to fail.

The equipment breakdown reveals systemic, not isolated, failures. Carts (197 mentions), ladders/OPs (190), and scanners (153) lead the categories, each representing a different operational bottleneck.

TMs used the words "broken" or "not working" 125 times and reported outright shortages 170 times. This isn't isolated equipment failure, it's preventative maintenance debt coming due.

MDT1 (17.0% equipment rate), PHX1 (14.7%), CLT1 (14.9%), and AVP2 (13.7%) require immediate equipment census and intervention. At these sites, equipment frustration has become a defining characteristic of the TM experience.

Insight

Equipment is the most controllable variable in this report. Unlike policy perception or facility constraints, broken carts and missing scanners can be fixed with capital allocation and maintenance discipline. The 2026 opportunity is treating equipment reliability as a frontline investment, not a facilities afterthought.

3. Facility Comfort & Housekeeping

Signals: ~2,808

The 2,808 facility signals surface a truth that operations teams sometimes miss: TMs judge organizational respect not by what happens on the production floor, but by what happens in the spaces designed for their comfort.

The breakroom and restroom aren't operational, they're personal. When a TM walks into a dirty bathroom or a breakroom with broken microwaves and overflowing trash, the message received isn't "facilities is behind." The message received is "you don't matter enough for us to maintain the spaces where you rest."

This is why facility signals carry disproportionate emotional weight. A malfunctioning scanner is frustrating; a filthy restroom is demoralizing. One affects productivity; the other affects dignity.

The specific breakdown reveals where facility friction concentrates. Restroom conditions (550 mentions) dominate, more than double the next category (breakroom at 280). Water leaks (203), parking (94), and trash accumulation (82) round out the top concerns.

RNO1 (14.4% facility rate), BNA1 (11.2%), and CFC1 (10.5%) carry the highest facility signal density. At RNO1, restroom cleanliness was mentioned 66 times, more than any other site. This is RNO1's most visible pain point despite the site's strong leadership culture.

Insight

Facility investment is culture investment. The 2026 priority isn't building new amenities, it's maintaining existing ones with visible accountability. Daily cleaning audits, posted schedules, and 48-hour amenity replacement SLAs will signal to TMs that their comfort matters as much as their output.

4. Policy Rigidity & Incentive Gaps

Signals: ~1,555

The 1,555 policy signals don't describe a broken system, they describe a system that TMs don't understand or trust. The distinction matters: fixing policy perception may not require changing policies at all, but rather changing how policies are communicated and applied.

The core frustration is opacity. TMs don't know how VTO is allocated. They don't understand why some people seem to get overtime opportunities while others don't. They watch the same behaviors result in different consequences depending on who's involved. Whether these perceptions reflect actual inconsistency or simply information gaps, the effect is the same: TMs feel the system is rigged.

The policy signal breakdown reveals where trust breaks down. VET/Overtime (726 mentions) leads overwhelmingly, followed by scheduling (354), VTO allocation (242), and compensation concerns (187). The fairness/favoritism category (143 explicit mentions) captures TMs directly naming inequity as the issue.

The September surge tells the story. Network-wide policy signals jumped from 197 in August to 310 in September, a 57% increase. This was Peak prep frustration boiling over. TMs felt the schedule pressure and perceived VTO/VET distribution as unfair.

MCI1 experienced the most dramatic spike: policy signals jumped from 29 in August to 64 in September (121% increase).

MCI1 (22.5% policy rate) and AVP1 (16.4%) require immediate intervention. At MCI1, nearly one in four signals relates to policy, this is a trust crisis, not a policy problem.

A secondary theme deserves attention: path equity. TMs in physically demanding paths (Dock, heavy pick zones) earn the same base rate as TMs in lighter paths. There's no financial recognition that Dock work is harder. The result is resentment: "Hard work is rewarded with more hard work" captures the sentiment. Multiple signals suggest that differential pay or point bonuses for demanding assignments would feel more equitable.

Insight

The 2026 priority is transparency, not policy overhaul. Publish VTO/VET criteria. Show allocation data. Host policy Q&As. When TMs understand the rules, they can accept outcomes they don't like. When they don't understand the rules, every outcome feels unfair.

5. Cross-Training & Labor Rotation Stagnation

Signals: ~353

The 353 rotation signals represent the smallest volume among the five opportunity themes, but they carry disproportionate emotional intensity. TMs who feel stuck in demanding paths aren't mildly frustrated, they're physically exhausted and mentally checked out. Rotation stagnation is a leading indicator for turnover.

The narrative is consistent across sites: TMs are assigned to a path (often a demanding one like Dock or heavy pick), they ask for rotation, and nothing changes. Weeks pass. Months pass. They watch other TMs rotate while they remain in the same physically taxing role. Whether this reflects actual favoritism or simply poor tracking systems, the perception is the same: the rotation system is unfair.

MCO1 and DAY1 carry the highest rotation signal density. At MCO1, 86 rotation signals include 36 specific mentions of Pack/Ship, TMs feel trapped in that path. At DAY1, 57 signals include 15 Dock mentions. These aren't isolated complaints; they're clusters of exhausted workers asking for relief.

The counter-signal is equally revealing: TMs want to be cross-trained. MCO1 recorded 50 mentions of training interest; DAY1 recorded 22. TMs aren't asking to avoid work, they're asking for variety and skill development. When that demand goes unmet, the message received is that their growth doesn't matter.

The root cause appears to be process, not intent. Rotation decisions happen at the AM level with manual tracking that's rarely audited. When rotations depend on individual manager memory and preference, inconsistency is inevitable. TMs who have good relationships with their AM get rotated; TMs who don't, stay stuck.

Insight

The 2026 opportunity is systematizing rotation. Implement time-in-path limits for demanding roles (4-hour maximum suggested). Create auditable rotation trackers at the site level. Cross-train TMs who request it. The goal isn't eliminating manager discretion, it's ensuring that discretion operates within fair, visible constraints.

Implement check-out/check-in protocols for high-loss items (scanners, handhelds) 4. Create shared replacement parts inventory across cluster 5. Assign dedicated equipment maintenance staffing (consider shared resource for AVP2/MDT1 given geographic proximity)

IX. Final Recommendations

  1. Don't treat this as 13 separate problems. The cluster approach reduces complexity from 13 site plans to 7 coordinated initiatives. Sites in the same cluster can share resources, playbooks, and accountability structures more efficiently.

  2. September is your early warning system. The 55% signal spike in September 2025 was TMs telling you Peak prep was painful. Pre-empt this in 2026 by addressing equipment, policy, and facility concerns in July–August.

  3. The VOC Board is necessary but insufficient. Sites with diversified listening (RNO1, MDT1, MCO1) surface nuanced issues early. DFW1 (an established site with only 2 mechanisms) represents a concerning regression that requires investigation. HOU1 (a newer site with 5 mechanisms) is still building its listening infrastructure, an expected growth phase, but one that should be accelerated.

  4. Policy perception ≠ policy reality. MCI1 and AVP1's policy signal rates reflect trust erosion, not necessarily unfair policies. The fix is transparency, not policy change.

  5. Facility conditions are a respect signal. TMs interpret dirty breakrooms as organizational apathy. The ROI on cleaning is higher than the cost suggests.

  6. Equipment is a promise. When TMs can't get scanners or functional carts, they perceive a broken contract: "You demand performance, but you don't give me tools."

  7. Rotation is a turnover leading indicator. MCO1's ergonomic signals and DAY1's rotation complaints are warning signs. Address path stagnation before it becomes attrition.
