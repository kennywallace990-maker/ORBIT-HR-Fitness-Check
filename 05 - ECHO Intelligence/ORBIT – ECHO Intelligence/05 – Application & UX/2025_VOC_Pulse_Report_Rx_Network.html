Executive Overview

This report provides a comprehensive, AI-driven thematic analysis of the 2025 employee listening data across the Chewy Rx Pharmacy Network. Drawing from 5,291 distinct TM signals across 7 active sites, its purpose is to distill raw feedback into actionable intelligence. The Rx Network operates fundamentally differently from the Core FC Network: these are smaller, climate-controlled facilities where accuracy, compliance, and pharmacist-technician collaboration define the daily experience. By isolating the top five cultural strengths and the bottom five operational opportunities, this document equips Rx leadership with the context needed to formulate targeted, high-impact action plans for 2026.

Network Composition: The 2025 Rx Network comprises seven pharmacy sites: SDF4, MCO4, PHX2, DFW4, AVP4, SDF6, and SDF2. SDF4, SDF6, and SDF2 account for 43% of total signal volume, making this the network's largest operating cluster.

Critical Note: SDF2 continues to operate without a VOC Board, which significantly limits signal capture at that site. SDF2's 480 signals represent the lowest volume in the Rx Network and likely understate the true scope of TM sentiment. Additionally, SDF4's data includes signals from former SDF1 TMs who transitioned during the 2024 closure, making the site's integration story a central thread in this analysis. Establishing a public feedback mechanism at SDF2 remains a foundational recommendation of this report.

With the expansion to seven sites and SDF4's absorption of the former SDF1 workforce, the network's total signal capture grew 37% year-over-year. Notably, SDF2 remains the only Rx site operating without a VOC Board, relying entirely on leader-mediated and structured channels.

II. Top 5 Positive Themes (Strengths to Leverage)

1. Connection to Work Outcomes

Signals: ~564 | Network-wide

The 564 positive signals in this theme show that Rx TMs understand the downstream effect of their work. Comments in this group consistently connect daily tasks to medication accuracy, patient safety, and the customer experience in a way that is more explicit than in the Core FC Network.

The language is specific rather than abstract. Pharmacy technicians describe catching dosing discrepancies before shipment, pharmacists describe the discipline required in verification and compounding, and support roles describe the importance of cold-chain accuracy and inventory handling. The common thread is that TMs can see how their work affects the quality of the final outcome.

MCO4 leads the network with 82 signals in this theme, nearly 9.2% of the site's total volume. SDF4 follows at 78, supported by the continuity of experienced technicians and pharmacists through the SDF1 transition. AVP4 generates 24 signals in the same theme, with newer hires consistently describing the work as more meaningful and more clinically grounded than prior pharmacy roles. The pattern is broad enough across all seven sites to treat this as a network characteristic rather than a site-specific outlier.

Week 3 surveys reinforce the same point. New hires frequently cite helping pets, medication accuracy, and a clearer connection between task execution and customer outcome as reasons they chose Chewy Pharmacy over other pharmacy environments.

Insight

This theme matters because it supports retention, onboarding, and training adoption. The practical implication for 2026 is straightforward: leaders should keep connecting daily work to medication accuracy, customer outcome, and quality standards without overstating the point. In the Rx network, that connection is already present in the signal data.

2. Pharmacist-Technician Collaboration

Signals: ~463 | Network-wide

The 463 collaboration signals describe something the Core FC Network rarely captures: genuine professional partnership between licensed pharmacists and pharmacy technicians. In the Rx Network, the pharmacist-tech relationship isn't hierarchical, it's symbiotic. Techs prepare, pharmacists verify. Techs flag anomalies, pharmacists investigate. The data reveals a workforce that thinks in clinical dyads rather than org chart boxes.

SDF4 drives the highest collaboration signal volume (142 signals), preserving the mentorship model that carried over from SDF1 before its 2024 closure. Former SDF1 TMs describe pharmacists who continued the "teach during verification" approach through the transition, turning quality checks into learning moments even while adapting to a new site's workflows. MCO4 follows at 87 signals, where the collaboration appears through pre-shift alignment that reduces surprises during dispensing runs.

The counter-signal is worth noting: where collaboration breaks down, TMs notice immediately. Signals from DFW4 and SDF2 include frustration when pharmacist verification queues back up and techs are left waiting, unable to advance work. SDF6's ramp-up exacerbates this, new techs describe feeling "stranded" when the pharmacist-to-tech ratio compresses during autoship weeks. The bottleneck isn't malicious, it's structural, but TMs experience it as a collaboration failure.

Insight

Pharmacist-tech collaboration is both a strength and a fragility. When ratios are adequate, the partnership flourishes. When staffing compresses, the collaboration turns into a bottleneck. The 2026 priority is staffing to protect the collaboration model, not just to meet volume. SDF4's preservation of SDF1's mentorship culture through a major site transition is a change management success worth documenting.

3. Leadership Accessibility in Small Buildings

Signals: ~393 | Network-wide

In the Core FC Network, leadership visibility is a hard-won achievement that requires deliberate walk programs and floor presence cadence. In the Rx Network, it's structural. Small pharmacy buildings mean the Site Lead, pharmacist-in-charge, and shift supervisors are physically steps away from every workstation. The 393 leadership signals describe TMs who feel their leaders are not just accessible but present by default.

This structural advantage creates a different leadership dynamic. TMs don't praise leaders for "walking the floor" because the floor is the office. Instead, they praise leaders for responsiveness: resolving a system issue within minutes, adjusting assignments when someone is struggling, and making real-time staffing calls when volume spikes.

SDF4 leads with 108 leadership signals, reflecting the site's larger headcount and the leadership team's handling of the SDF1 integration. MCO4 follows at 62, with TMs specifically praising leaders who maintain consistent communication routines during operational pressure. PHX2 at 52 and AVP4 at 42 have proportionally strong leadership density relative to their signal volumes. The newer sites (DFW4 at 38, SDF6 at 34, SDF2 at 28) have lower absolute volumes but similar leadership signal density, confirming this is a network characteristic, not a site-specific outlier.

Insight

Leadership accessibility is the Rx Network's structural advantage over larger FCs. Don't try to replicate Core FC walk programs, instead optimize what already works: rapid response, real-time problem-solving, and the intimacy that small buildings provide. The risk is that as sites grow (SDF4's post-merger headcount, DFW4's volume trajectory), this proximity erodes. Growth planning must protect the leadership accessibility ratio.

4. Schedule Stability & Work-Life Balance

Signals: ~336 | Network-wide

Pharmacy operations run on predictable cycles, and TMs value the resulting schedule stability deeply. The 336 signals in this category describe a workforce that has structured their personal lives around reliable pharmacy hours. Unlike the Core FC Network, where VTO/VET allocation is a major friction point, Rx Network TMs experience schedules as largely fixed and dependable.

The autoship cycle creates volume predictability that translates into workforce planning accuracy. TMs know when the busy periods are (beginning of month, autoship processing windows) and appreciate that leadership communicates volume expectations in advance.

New hires from retail pharmacy backgrounds are particularly vocal about this advantage. AVP4's signals are especially rich here, with repeated comparisons to less predictable retail schedules. "No more closing tonight and opening tomorrow" and "I actually have every other weekend off" capture the contrast. DFW4's TMs echo the same point. For pharmacy professionals leaving erratic retail scheduling models, Chewy's schedule stability is a meaningful differentiator.

Insight

Schedule stability is a recruitment differentiator worth protecting. As volume grows and the network expands, resist the temptation to introduce schedule variability. The pharmacy talent market is tight, and TMs will leave for environments that match Chewy's current predictability. Any schedule changes in 2026 should be communicated with maximum lead time. AVP4's and DFW4's new hire signals suggest this is the #1 factor in competitive pharmacy recruitment.

5. Training & Professional Development

Signals: ~271 | Network-wide

The 271 training signals tell a story unique to the Rx Network: TMs view their work as a career, not just a job. Pharmacy technicians describe Chewy's certification support, continuing education stipends, and cross-training opportunities as meaningful investments in their professional futures.

SDF4 and MCO4 drive the highest training signal volumes (76 and 58 respectively), reflecting mature training programs inherited and refined over time. SDF4's training infrastructure, a direct legacy of SDF1's structured onboarding and mentorship pairing, now serves as the template for newer sites. TMs specifically praise pharmacy-specific system onboarding, mentorship pairing with senior technicians, and the opportunity to learn compounding skills.

The demand signal is equally important: TMs want more. Signals from PHX2, DFW4, and SDF6 include requests for advanced certification paths, cross-training between dispensing and compounding, and opportunities to shadow pharmacists. SDF6's newest hires are the most vocal about career progression, asking "What does 'senior technician' look like here?" within their first quarter. This is a workforce hungry for growth, and unmet demand risks becoming attrition.

Insight

Training demand exceeds supply at newer sites. The 2026 priority is standardizing SDF4/MCO4's training programs across the network and creating visible career ladders for pharmacy technicians. When TMs see a path from entry-level tech to senior tech to lead, retention strengthens. SDF6 and AVP4 should be first in line for the formalized training rollout.

III. Bottom 5 Opportunity Themes (Areas for 2026 Action)

1. Workstation Ergonomics & Repetitive Strain

Signals: ~853

Ergonomics is the Rx Network's most frequently flagged concern, and the nature of the complaints is fundamentally different from the Core FC Network. While FC TMs report strain from heavy lifting and cart pushing, Rx TMs describe the cumulative toll of precision work: hours of standing at counting trays, repetitive hand motions during label application, neck strain from monitor positioning, and wrist fatigue from data entry.

The 853 ergonomic signals represent 16.1% of all Rx Network feedback, significantly higher than any single category in the Core FC report. The intensity of these signals is notable: TMs don't describe occasional discomfort, they describe chronic physical strain that compounds over shifts, weeks, and months.

The specific breakdown reveals where strain concentrates:

  • Standing fatigue: 256 mentions (no anti-fatigue mats at many stations)
  • Wrist/hand strain: 209 mentions (repetitive counting and labeling)
  • Neck/shoulder tension: 183 mentions (monitor height and keyboard positioning)
  • Back pain: 134 mentions (workstation height mismatches)
  • Eye strain: 71 mentions (small-print label verification)

SDF2 carries the highest ergonomic signal density at 22.1% of site total, followed by DFW4 at 16.9% and SDF6 at 16.8%. At SDF2, nearly one in four signals relates to physical strain, a rate that demands immediate intervention. SDF4's absolute volume (207 signals) is the highest in the network, reflecting post-merger workstation density issues as the former SDF1 workforce compressed into shared counter space.

Insight

Pharmacy ergonomics requires a different playbook than FC ergonomics. The fixes aren't about reducing load weight, they're about workstation design: adjustable-height counters, anti-fatigue mats, ergonomic keyboards, properly positioned monitors, and scheduled micro-breaks. The 2026 priority is a comprehensive workstation audit at all seven sites, starting with SDF2 (highest density) and SDF4 (highest volume). SDF6's standardized workstation layout needs task-specific differentiation between compounding, dispensing, and verification stations.

2. System & Software Reliability

Signals: ~789

The 789 system reliability signals describe a workforce whose throughput depends heavily on technology. Unlike the Core FC Network where equipment means physical tools (carts, scanners, ladders), the Rx Network's critical "equipment" is digital: the pharmacy management system (PMS), label printers, barcode verification systems, and inventory management software.

When the PMS crashes or slows, the entire pharmacy stops. Prescriptions can't be verified. Labels can't print. Inventory can't be updated. TMs describe the experience as "watching the queue grow while staring at a loading screen." The frustration is compounded by accountability: TMs are held to accuracy and throughput metrics that are impossible to meet when systems are down.

The specific breakdown:

  • PMS slowdowns/crashes: 271 mentions
  • Label printer jams/failures: 197 mentions
  • Barcode scanner errors: 122 mentions
  • Inventory system sync issues: 108 mentions
  • Computer/terminal performance: 91 mentions

DFW4 carries the highest system signal density at 18.2% of site total, followed by SDF2 at 17.1% and SDF6 at 15.4%. DFW4's elevated rate reflects infrastructure that hasn't scaled with the site's expanding prescription volume. SDF4's PMS complaints (72 mentions, highest absolute volume) reflect the complexity of processing combined prescription volumes from two legacy operations after the SDF1 merger.

Insight

System reliability is the Rx Network's equivalent of the Core FC's equipment crisis. The fix requires IT partnership: PMS performance monitoring, printer replacement cycles, terminal refresh schedules, and a rapid-response IT support model that treats pharmacy system downtime as a patient safety issue, not a help desk ticket. SDF4's PMS configuration needs a post-merger harmonization audit, and SDF6's system settings should be aligned with SDF4's proven workflows to enable flexible cross-site staffing in the Louisville corridor.

3. Temperature Control & Cold Chain Management

Signals: ~650

Temperature control in the Rx Network carries dual significance: it affects both TM comfort and product integrity. The 650 signals describe facilities where HVAC systems struggle to maintain comfortable working conditions while simultaneously preserving cold chain requirements for refrigerated and frozen medications.

The tension is operational. Cold storage areas require strict temperature maintenance for medication efficacy. But TMs who rotate between ambient workstations and cold storage describe temperature shock, inadequate cold-weather gear for refrigerated rooms, and HVAC systems that overcorrect, making the main floor uncomfortably cold to compensate for cold room proximity.

What makes this network's temperature story unique is its operating diversity. The sites face very different HVAC and seasonal pressures, which means the fixes need to be site specific rather than forced into one network wide playbook.

The specific breakdown:

  • HVAC inconsistency (too hot/too cold): 224 mentions
  • Cold storage discomfort: 168 mentions
  • Summer heat / winter cold in non-climate areas: 127 mentions
  • Cold chain equipment alarms: 79 mentions
  • Inadequate cold-weather PPE for cold rooms: 52 mentions

PHX2 carries the most extreme temperature signal density at 19.4% of site total, driven by HVAC demand that is overwhelming the building's capacity. MCO4 follows at 15.2% with persistent humidity and cold room balance issues. AVP4 at 13.6% shows the network's clearest winter temperature profile, with loading dock cold drafts, heating inconsistency, and dry air complaints peaking from November through February.

Insight

Temperature control in pharmacy is a compliance issue, not just a comfort issue. If TMs are uncomfortable in cold storage, they'll spend less time there, which increases operational risk. The 2026 priority is HVAC assessment at PHX2, MCO4, and AVP4, along with insulated PPE for cold storage rotations. Separating building comfort HVAC from cold-chain integrity systems is feasible in smaller buildings and should be evaluated network-wide.

4. Staffing & Workload Compression

Signals: ~584

The 584 staffing signals describe a network where small headcounts amplify every absence. In a Core FC with hundreds of TMs, one call-out is a minor inconvenience. In an Rx pharmacy with 15-25 TMs per shift, one absence cascades immediately: verification queues grow, dispensing slows, break schedules collapse, and remaining TMs absorb the workload.

The data reveals three distinct patterns:

Pattern 1: Chronic understaffing. Some sites consistently operate below optimal pharmacist-to-technician ratios. TMs describe shifts where a single pharmacist is responsible for verification volumes designed for two, creating bottlenecks that cascade through the entire operation.

Pattern 2: Spike compression. Autoship processing windows and beginning-of-month prescription surges create predictable volume spikes that the current staffing model doesn't fully accommodate. TMs describe "autoship week" as the most stressful period, where throughput expectations don't match available labor.

Pattern 3: Growth outpacing hiring. SDF6 and DFW4 are expanding faster than their staffing pipelines can fill. SDF6's pharmacist recruitment gap is the most acute example: the facility is built and equipped, but pharmacist coverage hasn't kept pace with prescription volume ramp-up.

  • Short-staffed shift mentions: 214 mentions
  • Pharmacist bottleneck/ratio: 134 mentions
  • Autoship/volume surge stress: 119 mentions
  • Break schedule disruption: 62 mentions
  • Overtime fatigue: 55 mentions

SDF6 carries the highest staffing signal density at 12.4% of site total, the most acute rate in the network and a direct consequence of pharmacist recruitment lagging prescription volume growth. SDF4 has the highest absolute staffing volume (154 signals), reflecting the larger workforce absorbing SDF1's transition. DFW4 at 11.7% follows SDF6's pattern of growth outpacing hiring.

Insight

Staffing in pharmacy is a precision problem. The fix isn't "more bodies," it's the right bodies at the right times. The 2026 priority is aligning pharmacist scheduling to volume forecasts (especially autoship windows), creating float pharmacist capacity for planned absences, and establishing minimum staffing thresholds below which sites escalate rather than absorb. SDF6 needs an accelerated pharmacist hiring pipeline with signing bonuses and relocation support. SDF4 should formalize cross-site pharmacist sharing with SDF6 and SDF2 in the Louisville corridor.

5. Facility Comfort in Compact Spaces

Signals: ~484

The 484 facility signals describe the unique challenge of small buildings. The Rx Network's facilities are a fraction of Core FC square footage, which creates intimacy (a strength) but also cramped conditions (an opportunity). Breakrooms are small. Restrooms are limited. Parking lots are tight. Personal space is scarce.

The emotional weight of these signals mirrors the Core FC pattern: TMs interpret facility neglect as organizational disrespect. But in small buildings, the effect is amplified. A dirty breakroom in a 500,000 sq ft FC is one room among many. A dirty breakroom in a 30,000 sq ft pharmacy is the room, the only space where TMs decompress.

  • Breakroom capacity/conditions: 154 mentions
  • Restroom conditions: 119 mentions
  • Workstation crowding: 86 mentions
  • Parking limitations: 68 mentions
  • Noise levels: 57 mentions

PHX2 carries the highest facility density at 10.3% of site total, where the compact footprint and desert climate make facility comfort a compound challenge. AVP4 follows at 9.5%, with winter parking lot conditions (ice, snow removal) adding a seasonal facility dimension unique to the northeast. SDF2 at 9.6% rounds out the top three, with breakroom capacity the primary driver.

Insight

Facility investment in small buildings has outsized ROI. Upgrading one breakroom at a pharmacy site costs a fraction of a Core FC renovation but impacts 100% of the workforce daily. The 2026 priority is breakroom refresh at PHX2 and SDF2, winter parking lot maintenance at AVP4 (reliable salt/plow contract), noise mitigation in open-plan dispensing areas at DFW4 and SDF4, and restroom cleaning cadence proportional to the smaller user base. PHX2's parking lot shade structures should be installed before summer 2026.

Implication for 2026

Schedule HVAC maintenance and cold room inspections in May. By July, it's too late. PHX2 and MCO4 specifically need HVAC capacity assessments before summer 2026. AVP4's aging system should be evaluated for both summer cooling and winter heating capacity.

Implication for 2026

AVP4's winter heating zone assessment should be completed by October 2025. Air curtains at loading docks, reliable parking lot snow/ice removal, and dry air mitigation are northeast-specific interventions that don't apply to the southern sites.

December Drop-Off December signals dropped to 264 (60% below September peak). This reflects reduced prescription volume during the holiday period and lower headcount due to PTO. The low December volume is expected and healthy, though the system signal count (63) remained disproportionately high, suggesting that end-of-year IT maintenance windows may create their own disruption.

SDF4

Total Signals: 1,347 | Unique Mechanisms: 9 | Network Anchor & SDF1 Integration Site

The SDF4 Story

SDF4 is the Rx Network's largest and most operationally complex site. When SDF1 closed in 2024, its team, processes, and institutional knowledge were absorbed into SDF4. The merger created the network's highest-volume pharmacy by headcount and signal count, and the data shows a site managing a major transition while maintaining core operating practices.

SDF4 now generates 1,347 signals (25.5% of all Rx Network feedback), and its collaboration profile is the strongest in the network. 142 collaboration signals describe the same mentorship-during-verification approach that defined SDF1, now operating at larger scale. Leadership signals (108) are the highest in the network, with TMs crediting site leaders for managing the integration with transparency, holding town halls to address concerns, and preserving shift preferences where possible.

But the merger amplified existing pain points. Ergonomic signals (207, or 15.4% of site total) reflect a facility absorbing more TMs into workstations designed for a smaller operation. Counter space is tighter, shared stations create scheduling friction, and the workstations that served SDF4's original headcount buckle under the combined workforce. System reliability (191 signals, 14.2%) mirrors the pattern: the PMS now processes combined prescription volumes from two legacy operations, and former SDF1 TMs describe configuration differences that create workflow confusion. Staffing (154 signals, 11.4%) is elevated above network average, with shift preference conflicts between "original SDF4" and "former SDF1" TMs surfacing in the data.

IX. Final Recommendations

  1. SDF2's VOC Board should be installed immediately. Every conclusion in this report involving SDF2 is limited by incomplete signal capture. Until SDF2 has a public feedback mechanism, leadership is making site-level decisions with partial information.

  2. Ergonomics is this network's defining challenge. The Rx Network doesn't have a cart problem or a ladder problem, it has a workstation problem. Anti-fatigue mats, adjustable counters, and proper monitor positioning aren't luxury items, they're tools that protect the accuracy and wellbeing of a precision workforce. SDF2's 22.1% density rate demands emergency intervention; SDF4's post-merger density demands capital investment; SDF6's uniform design demands task-specific differentiation.

  3. Standardize the shared operating cluster. SDF4, SDF6, and SDF2 share talent, workflows, and staffing pressure. PMS configurations, workstation standards, and training programs should be harmonized to enable cross-site workforce flexibility. A pharmacist who can seamlessly work at any of those sites is worth more than three site-specific pharmacists.

  4. System reliability is a patient safety issue. When the PMS crashes, prescriptions don't get verified. When labels don't print, medications don't ship. Treat pharmacy system downtime with the urgency of a clinical event, not a help desk ticket. DFW4's 18.2% system density and SDF4's 72 PMS slowdown mentions are the most urgent targets.

  5. PHX2's temperature crisis is a compliance risk. The 18 cold chain alarm mentions at PHX2 aren't just TM complaints, they're potential regulatory events. HVAC intervention at PHX2 should be treated as a compliance investment, not a facilities request. MCO4's humidity and AVP4's winter cold require parallel but climate-specific interventions.

  6. Accelerate SDF6's pharmacist pipeline. SDF6 has modern infrastructure sitting underutilized because pharmacist recruitment hasn't kept pace with prescription volume. Signing bonuses, relocation support, and temporary cross-site staffing from SDF4 should be deployed immediately. A beautiful pharmacy without enough pharmacists is just an expensive building.

  7. Preserve the strongest communication and training practices. MCO4's huddle structure and SDF4's mentorship-during-verification model are both repeatable practices that should be exported network-wide. These are practical operating habits, not abstract culture statements, and they can be replicated without major capital investment.

  8. Staff to protect collaboration, not just throughput. The pharmacist-tech partnership is one of the Rx Network's clearest strengths. When pharmacist ratios compress, collaboration becomes a bottleneck. Autoship-week staffing should be planned as carefully as holiday staffing in the Core FC Network. SDF6's pharmacist gap and MCO4's autoship-week strain are the most urgent examples.

  9. Small buildings amplify everything. In a 30,000 sq ft pharmacy, one dirty breakroom impacts everyone. One HVAC failure affects every workstation. One system crash stops the whole operation. The Rx Network's interventions do not need to be expensive, but they do need to be fast and visible. PHX2's parking shade, AVP4's winter lot maintenance, and SDF2's breakroom refresh each cost a fraction of a Core FC project while affecting the full site workforce.
