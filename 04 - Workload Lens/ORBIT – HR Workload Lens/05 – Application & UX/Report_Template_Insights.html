What’s Driving Our Ticket Volume —
and How We Stabilize It

Insights and recommendations from a two-week classification of 4,168 HR tickets integrated with UKG operational data
Prepared for HR Operations Leadership  |  March 10, 2026  |  Weeks 9–10 (Feb 22 – Mar 7, 2026)
Scope: Attendance Inquiry • CC Time & Attendance • FC General Inquiry • Timesheet Inquiry

Executive Summary

We classified all 4,168 tickets opened across four HR services during Weeks 9 and 10 to understand what is driving the week-to-week volume swings that appear in the OBR. The answer is clear: 60.3% of tickets (2,514) are contacts where the Chewtopian already had a self-service tool available — the UKG app, the timeclock, or Workday — but chose to call, email, or leave a voicemail instead. The two biggest drivers are TMs not using timeclocks for punches (818 tickets, 19.6% of all volume) and TMs calling in absences instead of using the UKG app (749 tickets, 18.0%). These two behaviors alone generate 1,567 tickets every two weeks and account for the majority of week-to-week variance. Meanwhile, UKG timecard audit data shows HR processes 67,543 manual actions per week underneath these tickets — work that is invisible to the OBR. The ticket queue is not a measure of HR workload; it is a measure of TM self-service adoption. The recommendations in this report focus on four areas: timeclock compliance, UKG app and desktop adoption for time-off and balance inquiries, targeted site campaigns at the highest-volume locations, and a system fix for the time-off coding defect that generates 250 tickets every two weeks. If executed, these actions can reduce weekly ticket volume by an estimated 18–30% and narrow the WoW variance that triggers recurring questions about what changed.

Focus Area 1: Missed Punches & Timeclock Compliance

The single largest ticket category — and the single largest UKG workload driver — is preventable.

Missed punches are the #1 ticket category and the #1 UKG workload driver across the network. When a TM fails to clock in or out, it creates two problems: first, the TM may submit a ticket asking HR to fix it (818 tickets over two weeks); second, HR proactively corrects the timecard regardless of whether a ticket is filed (32,693 corrections per week in UKG). The ticket represents only 2.5% of the actual correction workload — for every one missed-punch ticket, HR silently fixes approximately 80 others.

This category is remarkably stable week over week (410 vs. 408), meaning it is not driving the variance — it is the structural floor underneath the variance. It makes the baseline high, so that every fluctuation in other categories looks proportionally larger in the OBR.

The solution is straightforward: these TMs had a timeclock available and did not use it.

Recommendation: Launch a targeted timeclock compliance communication campaign at the top-volume sites (see Focus Area 4). Reinforce timeclock usage through shift-start huddle reminders, visible signage near entry points, and manager accountability for shift-level punch compliance. For CC/CVC locations where physical timeclocks may be less accessible, promote desktop UKG access for clocking in/out at workstation start and end of shift.

Expected impact: A 25% improvement in timeclock usage would eliminate ~100 tickets/week and ~8,000 UKG manual corrections/week, returning significant capacity to local HR and WFM teams.

This is the category that swings the most week to week. Phone call outs dropped 20.6% from Week 9 to Week 10, accounting for 45% of the total 244-ticket decline. Why? Because call-off volume is inherently unpredictable — a weather event, a flu outbreak, or a quiet week all change how many TMs call in on a given day. There is no intake structure governing this channel, so it fluctuates naturally.

Weather absences alone generated 162 tickets across both weeks (81 per week, perfectly stable), all classified as self-service eligible — TMs can report absences through the UKG app regardless of the reason.

Recommendation: Drive adoption of the UKG mobile app for absence and call-off submissions. TMs should be trained and reminded that they can submit call outs, time-off requests, and absence notifications directly through the app — no phone call needed. For Chewtopians who prefer desktop, promote desktop UKG access as an alternative self-service channel for submitting time-off requests and reviewing schedules.

Pair this with a communication campaign at the start of each shift reminding TMs of the self-service options: “Need to call out? Use the UKG app. Need to request PTO? Use UKG or Workday. No phone call necessary.”

Expected impact: Even a 30% adoption shift would deflect ~110 call-out tickets per week and significantly reduce the Monday peak (when absence reporting volume is highest), narrowing WoW variance by approximately 40%.

PTO/UTO balance inquiries grew 64.9% from Week 9 to Week 10, making this the fastest-growing self-service sub-category. Beyond the 98 tickets specifically tagged as balance inquiries, UTO confusion appears across hundreds of Attendance Inquiry tickets where TMs reference unexpected deductions or negative balances.

The root cause is visibility. UKG data shows 4,869 manual UTO deduction actions per week across FC, CC, and Rx. Every one of those manual coding events is an opportunity for a Chewtopian to see an unexpected balance change on their next pay stub and pick up the phone to ask HR what happened. The information is technically available in the UKG app and Workday, but TMs either do not know where to find it or do not trust what they see.

Time-off requests by email and phone add another 314 tickets over two weeks (TM time-off requests via email: 76, phone TM time requests: 238) — requests that could have been submitted directly through UKG or desktop UKG access.

Recommendation: Promote UKG app and desktop UKG access as the primary channels for TMs to submit time-off requests and review their PTO/UTO balances. Specific actions:

(a) Include UKG balance-check instructions in new hire onboarding and annual refresher communications.
(b) Post QR codes to the UKG app at site break rooms and time clocks with a simple message: “Check your PTO/UTO balance anytime — no need to call HR.”
(c) For CC/CVC Chewtopians, ensure desktop UKG access is bookmarked on workstations with a one-click path to balance lookup and time-off submission.

Expected impact: Deflecting even 20% of balance and time-off request tickets would remove ~50 tickets/week from the queue and address the fastest-growing demand category before it scales further.

Defects are a small category (359 tickets, 8.6% of total), but they have an outsized impact on TM trust. Improper time-off coding accounts for 250 of 359 defect tickets (69.6%), concentrated in CC Time & Attendance (which carries 73.8% of all defects). These are cases where the NICE/UKG integration applied an incorrect time-off code, resulting in a wrong deduction or balance error. When a Chewtopian sees an incorrect deduction on their timecard, they lose trust in the system — and the next time they need to check a balance or submit time off, they call HR instead of self-serving. Defects erode the very self-service adoption we need to grow.

Recommendation: Engage the NICE/UKG integration team to audit the time-off coding logic that generates these errors. This is a system configuration fix, not a people or process fix. Resolving it would eliminate ~125 defect tickets per week and — equally important — remove a trust barrier to self-service adoption.

The Bigger Picture: What Tickets Don’t Show

Ticket volume is the tip of the iceberg. UKG data reveals the full HR workload underneath.

Everything in this report describes the 4,168 tickets that Chewtopians created. But tickets only capture what TMs ask about. UKG timecard audit data shows what HR actually does — and the gap is enormous.

During Week 9, the HR network processed 67,543 timecard actions consuming an estimated 761 FTE hours. Of those, 22,885 were rework actions (corrections, edits, retroactive fixes). The 1,962 tickets represent roughly 8% of that rework. The other 92% happens proactively, silently, with no OBR visibility.

The implication: when ticket volume drops 11.1% in a week, it does not mean HR workload dropped. UKG shows the same ~67,500 actions consuming ~761 FTE hours regardless of ticket swings. What changed is how many TMs chose to pick up the phone. The OBR is measuring TM behavior, not HR effort.

Reporting recommendation: Add three elements to the weekly OBR to make variance immediately interpretable without ad hoc analysis: (1) separate automation vs. manual ticket volume, (2) include a self-service classification breakdown, and (3) add a UKG Rework Coverage metric (currently 8%). This shifts the conversation from “why did volume change?” to “what are we doing to drive self-service adoption?”

The Bottom Line

The week-to-week variance in our ticket volumes is not random and it is not a sign of operational instability. It is the predictable result of Chewtopian self-service behavior — specifically, how many TMs choose to call HR instead of using the timeclock, UKG app, or Workday on a given week. Sixty percent of our tickets are self-service eligible today. The tools exist. The opportunity is adoption.

The four focus areas in this report — timeclock compliance, UKG app and desktop adoption for absences and time-off, balance visibility, and targeted site campaigns — are not aspirational. They are actionable, measurable, and tied directly to the data. If we execute on them, we reduce baseline volume by 18–30%, narrow the WoW variance, and shift the OBR conversation from “what happened this week?” to “how much closer are we to our self-service targets?”

That is a fundamentally better question to be answering.
