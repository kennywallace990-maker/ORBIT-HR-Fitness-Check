## What We Found

We audited two weeks of team member timecards across all 26 FC and Rx locations (3/1–3/14/2026) using a direct export from UKG Pro. Of the 10,731 team members with time-off entries during this period, we identified **1,488 timecard defects still live on team member records** — instances where more time-off was applied than the gap between the employee's schedule and what they actually worked.

These are not theoretical. Each one is a team member's timecard that is currently wrong and hasn't been corrected. The time-off on their record exceeds what should have been applied.

**The important part**: This report doesn't say "here are 1,488 problems — go fix them." The real value is in identifying *why* these defects happen and measuring their frequency, so leadership can form plans and take actions to prevent defects from happening in the first place.

## Why Paycode Defects Matter

Not all over-applications carry the same risk. The consequences depend on the type of time-off involved.

### PTO (Paid Time Off) — Highest Risk

Over-applied PTO is the most consequential defect because it touches pay, benefits, and policy simultaneously:

- **Incorrect PTO balance**: Team members see the wrong available PTO in UKG Pro. They may believe they've used time they haven't, or vice versa, leading to denied PTO requests or surprise balances.
- **Payroll defects**: When over-applied PTO is discovered after a pay period closes, payroll must process a correction — deducting from a future check, creating a negative adjustment, and generating documentation. This is the only paycode category that creates a direct pay defect.
- **Inconsistent attendance policy**: Over-applied PTO can mask actual absence patterns. One team member gets points, another doesn't, for the same behavior.
- **Reinstatement risk**: PTO accrual or balance thresholds can trigger policy actions. An incorrect balance can cause or prevent reinstatements incorrectly.
- **Poor employee experience**: Team members spend time in the HR office, on hold with HRSS, or submitting tickets to resolve a problem they didn't create. This is stress, rework, and lost productive time — for the team member and for HR.

### UTO (Unpaid Time Off) — Same Operational Risk, No Pay Defect

Over-applied UTO carries all the same consequences except the payroll correction:
- Incorrect attendance records and wrong point accumulation
- Points can trigger progressive discipline — an incorrectly applied UTO absence point could be the difference between a coaching conversation and a termination
- Inconsistent policy application and HR rework

### VTO (Voluntary Time Off) — Lowest Risk, Still Impacts Metrics

VTO over-application is the least harmful to individual team members, but it distorts building-level metrics:
- Inflates total time-off hours and reduces reported hours scheduled
- Deflates attendance rates and utilization metrics
- Can skew staffing models and workforce planning

VTO defects are lower priority for individual correction but should still be tracked for metric integrity.

## Root Cause Analysis — Priority Stack Rank

This is the most important section. Detection and correction are necessary, but the highest-value outcome is understanding *why* defects happen so we can prevent them upstream.

We analyzed every flagged defect to understand what paycodes were involved, whether they were applied by automation (RPA) or manually, and whether multiple paycodes were stacked on the same employee/date. Here is what the data shows, stack-ranked by impact.

---

### Priority 1: PTO + RPA Paycode Stacking — ~37% of Minor defects, highest hours impact

**What's happening**: When a team member has pre-scheduled PTO *and* also arrives late or leaves early, the RPA applies an automatic paycode (`Personal UNPD Late arrival` or `Personal UNPD Early Departure`) on top of the existing PTO without checking what's already there. The combined time-off exceeds the available schedule gap.

**Scale**: 3,175 of the Minor defect instances involve PTO + RPA stacking. This is the single largest driver of over-applied hours in the dataset.

**Example**: A team member has 4 hrs PTO pre-scheduled on a 10-hr shift (6 hrs remaining). They arrive 3 hrs late. RPA applies 3 hrs late arrival. Total time-off = 7 hrs against a 6-hr gap = 1 hr over-applied.

**Why it matters**:
- The PTO portion creates pay and balance consequences (see PTO risk above)
- The stacking makes the defect harder for local HR to spot — each paycode looks correct in isolation, but together they exceed the schedule
- This pattern is invisible without cross-referencing schedule, PTO, and RPA paycodes together — exactly what the reconciler does

**Insight for leadership**: The RPA workflow currently operates in isolation. It calculates the gap between punch and schedule but does not query whether PTO or other time-off paycodes already cover part of that gap. This is a design limitation in the automation, not a user error.

**Recommendation**: Evaluate whether the RPA logic can be updated to query existing scheduled PTO before applying automatic paycodes. If PTO already covers part of the gap, the RPA-applied paycode should be reduced accordingly.

**Owner**: RPA team

---

### Priority 2: RPA Meal Break Gap — 42% of all defects, highest instance count

**What's happening**: The RPA workflow that applies `Personal UNPD Early Departure` and `Personal UNPD Late arrival` paycodes calculates the gap between the team member's punch time and their scheduled shift start/end. However, it does not deduct the 30-minute unpaid meal break from this calculation.

**Scale**: 627 instances. Every affected instance shows exactly 0.45–0.55 hrs of over-application — the meal break. This is the most frequent single defect pattern.

**Example**: A team member is scheduled 6:30am–5:00pm (10.5 hrs gross, 10.0 hrs net after meal break). They leave at 4:30pm. The actual missed work is 0 minutes (they worked the full net shift). But the RPA calculates 5:00pm - 4:30pm = 0.5 hrs and applies a 0.5 hr early departure paycode.

**Why we stack-ranked this #2 despite being 42% of instances**: Each individual instance is only ~0.5 hrs. The per-instance impact is low compared to PTO stacking (avg 1.45 hrs) or excess entries (avg 6+ hrs). However, the aggregate volume is significant (314 total hours) and this is the single easiest fix — one automation change eliminates all 627 instances.

**Insight for leadership**: This is a systemic automation defect, not a manual entry error. 40.1% of meal break flags have `Personal UNPD Early Departure` (17.3%) or `Personal UNPD Late arrival` (22.8%) as the paycode, confirming RPA origin.

**Recommendation**: Update the early departure and late arrival RPA calculation to subtract the 30-minute meal break for shifts >= 6 hours. This single change would eliminate 42% of all detected defects.

**Owner**: RPA team

---

### Priority 3: Single-Paycode PTO Over-Entry — 34% of Minor defects

**What's happening**: A single PTO entry (no stacking, no RPA involvement) exceeds the available schedule gap. This is typically a manual entry by the team member, manager, or HR where the PTO hours were entered incorrectly — either the wrong duration or the wrong date.

**Scale**: 2,933 instances in the Minor bucket, 245 in the Excess bucket. Average over-application: 1.49 hrs (Minor), 6.29 hrs (Excess).

**Why it matters**: These are the defects most likely to create payroll corrections and incorrect PTO balance reporting, because the over-application is entirely PTO.

**Insight for leadership**: Unlike the RPA-driven defects (Priorities 1 and 2), these originate from manual data entry in UKG Pro. They cannot be prevented by automation logic changes alone. They require either:
- Faster detection (Daily People Pulse catching them within 24 hrs)
- Entry-time validation (UKG Pro rules that flag suspicious entries before they save)

**Recommendation**: 
- **Near-term**: Daily People Pulse will surface these to local HR within 24 hours of occurrence, enabling same-day correction before the pay period closes.
- **Medium-term**: Explore UKG Pro validation rules to flag PTO entries where the hours exceed the remaining schedule gap, or where a single PTO entry exceeds 12 hours (AM/PM error detection).

**Owner**: Local HR (detection), UKG configuration team (prevention)

---

### Priority 4: Single-Paycode RPA Over-Application — 11% of Minor defects

**What's happening**: An RPA-applied paycode (`Personal UNPD Late arrival`, `Personal UNPD Early Departure`, `Personal UNPD Call Off`, or `Personal UNPD NCNS`) exceeds the available schedule gap, even without any other paycode present.

**Scale**: 933 instances. Average over-application: 0.62 hrs.

**Why it matters**: These are smaller than the PTO-driven defects but they affect attendance records. Over-applied unpaid time-off paycodes can incorrectly accumulate absence points.

**Insight for leadership**: The RPA is calculating the correct gap between punch and schedule end, but in some cases the schedule in UKG has been updated after the RPA applied the paycode (e.g., schedule changed from 10 hrs to 8 hrs, but the RPA had already applied time-off based on the 10-hr schedule). The reconciler catches the discrepancy by comparing against the current schedule.

**Recommendation**: This is a timing issue — the RPA runs before schedule finalization in some cases. The Daily People Pulse will catch these within 24 hours. No automation change is recommended unless the frequency increases.

**Owner**: Local HR (detection via Daily People Pulse)

---

### Priority 5: AM/PM Miscoding — Rare, high per-instance impact

**What's happening**: PTO end time entered as PM instead of AM, inflating duration by ~12 hours.

**Scale**: 2 instances in this period. Average over-application: 16.04 hrs per instance.

**Recommendation**: The Daily People Pulse will surface these to local HR within 24 hours. A UKG Pro validation rule flagging PTO entries > 12 hours could prevent these at entry time.

**Owner**: Local HR (detection), UKG configuration team (prevention)

## Data & Methodology

### Data Source

| Element | Source | Why |
|---------|--------|-----|
| Time-off applied | UKG Pro CSV export (direct) | Eliminates deleted-row ambiguity in Snowflake data warehouse |
| Worked hours | UKG Pro CSV export (direct) | Same — no batch-load artifacts |
| Scheduled hours | Snowflake (GOLD_V_SCHEDULE_TOTAL, V_SCHEDULE_SHIFT) | Schedule tables are reliable — append-only, versioned |
| Schedule history | Snowflake (V_SCHEDULE_TRANSACTION) | For detecting schedule overwrites |

The production reconciler uses Snowflake timecard tables, which have a known issue where deleted rows can't be reliably identified. For this validation, we bypassed that entirely by pulling a direct export from UKG Pro for the full two-week period.

### Scope

| Metric | FC | Rx | Total |
|--------|----|----|-------|
| Buildings | 13 | 13 | 26 |
| Team members | 9,618 | 1,113 | 10,731 |
| Employee-days with time-off | 51,675 | 4,996 | 56,671 |

### Paycode Exclusions

Certain leave categories are managed outside normal time-off reconciliation. Including them in the calculation inflated defect counts by 74% with non-actionable flags. We validated this by analyzing 755 mixed-paycode days.

| Excluded Paycode | Why |
|-----------------|-----|
| LEAVE / FMLA | Protected leave managed by Leave Administration. Not correctable through normal HR channels. |
| Intermittent Leave (PTO & Unpaid) | FMLA-certified. Managed by Leave Administration and a healthcare provider. |
| Bereavement / Bereavement - Unpaid | Compassionate leave. Managed through Leave Administration. |
| Military Leave | USERRA-protected. Managed by the Military Leave team. |

These paycodes are excluded from the over-application calculation but still appear in the paycode detail column when they co-occur with non-exempt paycodes, preserving full context for auditors.

### Human-in-the-Loop Audit

K. Wallace (People Analytics) individually audited 80 timecards from the flagged output, stratified across FC and Rx buildings and across root cause categories. For each record: pulled up the UKG Pro timecard, verified scheduled hours against the shift, verified punch times, verified each paycode entry, independently calculated the expected over-application, and compared against the reconciler output.

**Result: 80 out of 80 confirmed as true defects. Zero false positives.**

## Statistical Validation (SPC)

### In Plain Language

We checked 80 flagged timecards by hand. Every single one was a real problem. Statistically, that means we're at least 95.5% confident the tool is right across the board — not just in the 80 we checked, but across all 1,488 flags and beyond.

### The Framework

We used a **p-chart** (proportion defective chart) — the standard SPC tool for monitoring the proportion of a process output that meets a specification. Each flagged record is a binary outcome: true defect or false positive. The p-chart tells us whether the detection logic is producing consistent, reliable results or degrading in certain conditions.

### Confidence Interval (Clopper-Pearson Exact)

With n = 80 and x = 80 (zero false positives), the normal approximation fails. We use the Clopper-Pearson exact method based on the Beta distribution:

```
p_lower = Beta(0.025, 80, 1) = 0.9554
```

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Sample size (n) | 80 |
| Observed defects (x) | 80 |
| Point estimate (p-hat) | 1.000 |
| 95% lower confidence bound | **0.9554** |

We are 95% confident the true detection accuracy is at least 95.5%. The actual rate is almost certainly higher.

### Ongoing Monitoring

| Action | Frequency | Trigger |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Re-audit 30–50 records | Monthly | Standard monitoring |
| Plot p-hat on p-chart | Monthly | Track for degradation |
| Investigate | If p-hat < 0.90 | Process may be degrading |
| Full re-validation | When RPA logic changes | RPA owns 42%+ of defect volume |

## Appendix C: Technical Reference (Engineering)

This section is for engineers implementing, maintaining, or extending the detection logic.

### C.1 Over-Application Formula

```sql
effective_scheduled = CASE
    WHEN scheduled_hours = 0 AND original_from_history > 0
    THEN original_from_history
    ELSE scheduled_hours
END

over_applied = MAX(0,
    timeoff_applied_hours         -- non-exempt paycodes only
    - scheduled_pto               -- PTO already on the schedule
    - MAX(0, effective_scheduled - worked_hours)
)
```

### C.2 Key Rules

| Rule | Logic | Purpose |
|------|-------|---------|
| Worked >= Scheduled | `IF worked >= effective_scheduled THEN 0` | Full shift worked = no over-application |
| De minimis | `IF over_applied < 0.0334 THEN 0` | Eliminate UKG rounding noise (< 2 min) |
| Flagging threshold | `over_applied > 0.25` | Only surface defects >= 15 minutes |
| Exempt exclusion | `SUM(CASE WHEN NOT is_exempt THEN hrs ELSE 0 END)` | FMLA, bereavement, military excluded from hours |
| Exempt-only filter | `has_non_exempt_timeoff = 1 AND timeoff > 0` | Rows with only exempt paycodes excluded entirely |

### C.3 Exempt Paycodes

```sql
('LEAVE', 'timeoff', FALSE, TRUE),
('Intermittent Leave PTO', 'timeoff', TRUE, TRUE),
('Intermittent Leave-Unpaid', 'timeoff', FALSE, TRUE),
('Bereavement', 'timeoff', FALSE, TRUE),
('Bereavement - Unpaid', 'timeoff', FALSE, TRUE),
('Military Leave', 'timeoff', FALSE, TRUE)
```

Excluded from `timeoff_applied_hours` and `pto_hours_applied`. Included in `paycodes_detail` (LISTAGG) for context.

### C.4 Schedule Version Selection

| CTE | Order | Why |
|-----|-------|-----|
| `schedule_original` | `DESC` (latest) | Current schedule |
| `shift_hours` | `DESC` (latest) | Current shift for meal break deduction |
| `schedule_pto` | `DESC` (latest) | Current scheduled PTO |
| `schedule_original_from_history` | `ASC` (earliest) | Pre-overwrite schedule for overwrite detection |

### C.5 Root Cause Classification (CASE order matters)

```sql
1. NCNS + Call Off             -- RPA workflow incomplete
2. 0.45-0.55 hr band          -- Meal break not deducted
3. AM/PM confirmed             -- Transaction-level timestamp evidence
4. TIMEOFF_OVERWROTE_SCHEDULE  -- Schedule replaced by time-off
5. UNSCHEDULED_DAY_TIMEOFF     -- Time-off on unscheduled day
6. 11-13 hr band              -- Likely AM/PM error
7. timeoff >= 15 + over >= 10 -- Possible AM/PM error
8. >= 4 hrs                   -- Excess time-off
9. > 0.25 hrs                 -- Minor over-application
```
